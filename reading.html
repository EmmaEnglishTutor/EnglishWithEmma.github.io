Reading & research

Why reading matters, and why one-to-one teaching works.

A short, sourced collection on reading for pleasure and the evidence behind individual tuition — for any parent who wants the case for it, not just my word for it. Every source below is linked directly.

On reading

The case for reading, from the people who study it.

Michael Rosen has spent decades arguing for reading as something worth doing for its own sake, not just for the exam. Recent national data shows why that argument matters more than ever.

Michael RosenBlog, 2019
A 20-point plan for reading for pleasure

Rosen sets out practical ways adults can build a genuine reading culture around children — from reading aloud regularly to leaving room for real conversation about books, rather than treating every book as a comprehension test.

Read the source →
Michael RosenBlog, 2021
Reading for pleasure — how does it work its magic?

Rosen explores how the structure of stories — sequence, cause and effect, the slow reveal of a plot — trains a kind of thinking that goes well beyond the page, and why wide, frequent reading matters as much as any single book.

Read the source →
National Literacy Trust2025
Children and young people's reading in 2025

Based on responses from over 114,000 children and young people, this report found reading enjoyment at its lowest level in twenty years — but also that pupils who enjoy reading are more than twice as likely to have above-average reading skills.

Read the source →
National Literacy TrustOngoing
Reading for Pleasure hub

The Trust's ongoing research programme, linking reading enjoyment to wider outcomes including wellbeing, empathy and long-term academic success, alongside practical guidance for building a stronger reading culture at home and at school.

Read the source →
On one-to-one tuition

What the evidence says about individual teaching.

One-to-one tuition is one of the most consistently well-evidenced interventions in education research, across independent academic studies and UK government-funded evaluations alike.

Education Endowment FoundationToolkit, updated 2025
One to one tuition — Teaching & Learning Toolkit

The EEF's toolkit, drawn from a meta-analysis of controlled studies, rates one-to-one tuition as high impact for moderate cost, with pupils typically making several additional months of progress compared with those who don't receive it.

Read the source →
Nickow, Oreopoulos & QuanReview of Educational Research, 2024
The Promise of Tutoring for PreK–12 Learning

This systematic review and meta-analysis of experimental tutoring studies found consistently substantial positive effects on learning, with the strongest results for programmes delivered by qualified teachers rather than volunteers or parents.

Read the source →
NFER, for the Department for Education2024
National Tutoring Programme Year 3: Impact Evaluation

This independent evaluation of England's National Tutoring Programme found tutoring associated with measurable improvements in pupil outcomes, with school leaders also reporting a strong, consistent perception of impact on pupils' confidence.

Read the source →
